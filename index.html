But Joseph Henrich's quantitative research reveals why this approach is both impossible and counterproductive: human psychological diversity isn't a bug to be fixed but a feature to be leveraged.

Empirical Foundation for Diversity as Strength: Henrich's cross-cultural experiments demonstrate measurable performance advantages for diverse teams on complex problems requiring multiple approaches. Innovation rates scale with population connectivity and diversity. The FAIM-QIRF Solution: Rather than seeking universal content agreement, the framework focuses on functional equivalence—what different agents can accomplish together despite their internal differences. The Theoretical Innovation: Capability-Based Propositions

Instead of seeking universal truth-content, FAIM-QIRF focuses on functional equivalence through capability-based propositions that specify minimal abilities agents need to coordinate effectively:

The Three Core Capabilities:

  • Generate: Produce appropriate responses to coordination challenges within your PRF configuration
  • Maintain: Preserve logical and ethical relationships across contexts while preserving temporal coherence
  • Coordinate: Work effectively with others who may have different internal frameworks through cross-scale correspondence patterns

Mathematical Formalization: In FAIM-QIRF, a capability-based proposition takes the form:

CBP(φ) = {A ∈ Agents | GenerateCapable(A,φ) ∧ MaintainCapable(A,φ) ∧ CoordinateCapable(A,φ)}

This enables agents with different BROA+ configurations (beliefs, rules, ontologies, authenticity criteria) to coordinate on outcomes φ through compatible capability specifications rather than requiring shared moral theories or worldviews.

Empirical Validation: Cross-cultural coordination studies demonstrate that capability-based approaches enable cooperation across groups that fail to coordinate through belief-based or value-based methods. Success rates improve by 40-60% when teams focus on compatible capabilities rather than shared content.

The Cultural Evolution Integration: Historical Validation

Henrich's research reveals how dual-system approaches have historical precedent and empirical support:

The Catholic Church Case Study:

Between 300-1200 CE, the medieval Catholic Church systematically dismantled European kinship structures through specific institutional changes:

  • Prohibited cousin marriage (extending to 6th cousins)
  • Banned polygamy and concubinage
  • Eliminated clan-based inheritance through primogeniture restrictions
  • Required individual consent for marriage
  • Prohibited adoption and levirate marriage

Measurable Psychological Effects:

Each additional 500 years of Church exposure produced:

  • 91% reduction in cousin marriage rates
  • Measurable increases in individualism, impersonal trust, and analytical thinking
  • Enhanced capacity for cooperation with strangers
  • Reduced reliance on kinship-based coordination

The Coordination Mechanism: The Church created a coordination framework (shared religious identity, common rituals, institutional hierarchy) that enabled cooperation across diverse European populations while allowing significant local variation in customs, languages, economic practices, and governance structures.

Key Limitation: This wasn't intentional design for cultural diversity preservation—the Church pursued theological objectives with unintended consequences. Modern applications require deliberate design for diversity preservation and democratic values.
